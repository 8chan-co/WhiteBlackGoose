Why YYYY-MM-DD date format is the best

I keep seeing Europeans and Americans arguing on reddit whether it should be DD/MM/YYYY or MM/DD/YYYY. I've always disagreed with both, let me give you a few reasons why. *Disclaimer: the arguments here are mostly for people who read from the left*



1. Consistency with other measurements

First of all, how do we write numbers? Take **645**. Its first (reading from the left) digit is **6**, the most significant digit. So when you consider a price of a product, you most likely will care more about its major units than those minor (e. g. "six hundred dollars" is not much different from "six hundred fourty five").

Now, let's take something else. For example, time, which we write as "15:45" and read starting from its major units. See the pattern?

In **yyyy-mm-dd** it's the same. Starting from the most major unit, ending with a minormost one.

2. Universality for daily usage

An argument for MM/DD/YYYY and DD/MM/YYYY is that they're supposedly easier to work with for people in everyday life. I disagree with it.

What is our use of date and time? Surely the most basic option would be the precise moment in time, e. g. year 2022, month 10, day 20, hour 12, minute 16, second 10. But it's impractical for daily life, it's long, inconvenient, error-prone.

Our universal format allows to *cut* any continuous interval from ISO8601 (**YYYY-MM-DD HH:mm:ss**) which still makes perfect sense. For example, you want to hold a party soon, so you cut (YYYY-**MM-DD HH**:mm:ss). Or you want to research when Napoleon started working on his Napoleonic plans, which would be (**YYYY-MM**-DD HH:mm:ss). Or a lecture starts soon, you're perhaps interested in (YYYY-MM-DD HH:**mm:ss**). Likewise, a full date and a full time are also continuous intervals taken from this universal format!
